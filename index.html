The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints
An LDS mission refers to a mission served by a member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS Church). Young men and women who are members of the LDS Church have the opportunity to serve a full-time mission, usually for a period of 18 to 24 months, in various locations around the world. The purpose of an LDS mission is to share the teachings of the LDS Church, provide service to others, and invite individuals to learn about and join the LDS Church. Missionaries are typically assigned to a specific area or region and spend their time teaching, serving, and living according to the principles of their faith. Serving a mission is considered a voluntary and honorable opportunity for members of the LDS Church to share their beliefs and contribute to the growth of their faith community.
1. Faith and Service: Many individuals feel a strong sense of faith and a desire to share their beliefs with others. Serving a mission is seen as a way to fulfill a religious duty and share the teachings of the LDS Church with those who may be interested. 2. Personal Growth: A mission is often viewed as a time for personal growth and development. Missionaries have the opportunity to learn new languages, develop leadership skills, and gain a deeper understanding of their faith. 3. Building Relationships: Serving a mission allows individuals to build relationships with people from diverse backgrounds and cultures. Missionaries often form close bonds with those they serve and with other missionaries they serve alongside. 4. Strengthening Commitment: Going on a mission is seen as a way to strengthen one’s commitment to their faith. It is a time for individuals to deepen their understanding of LDS teachings and principles. 5. Making a Difference: Many missionaries are motivated by a desire to make a positive difference in the lives of others. They see their mission as an opportunity to serve and help those in need. Overall, going on a two-year LDS mission is a deeply personal decision that is influenced by a variety of factors. For many, it is a time of growth, learning, and service that has a lasting impact on their lives.
1. Morning: Missionaries usually wake up early, around 6:30 or 7:00 a.m. They begin the day with personal and companionship study, where they study the scriptures, other religious texts, and materials provided by the LDS Church. This study is meant to deepen their understanding of the gospel and prepare them for the day’s activities. 2. Breakfast: After study, missionaries have breakfast together with their companions. Meals are typically simple and nutritious. 3. Missionary Work: The bulk of the day is spent engaged in missionary work, which can include a variety of activities such as: • Proselytizing: Going door-to-door or approaching people in public places to share their message and invite them to learn more about the LDS Church. • Teaching: Meeting with individuals or groups who are interested in learning about the LDS Church and its teachings. Missionaries often teach lessons from the scriptures and share personal testimonies. • Service: Engaging in community service projects to help those in need and build relationships with community members. • Church Activities: Attending and participating in LDS Church meetings, such as Sunday worship services and midweek activities. 4. Lunch: Missionaries typically have a quick lunch break during the day to refuel before continuing their work. 5. Afternoon: Missionary work continues in the afternoon, with a focus on finding and teaching individuals who are interested in learning about the LDS Church. 6. Evening: In the evening, missionaries have dinner together and often participate in additional study and planning for the next day’s activities. They may also have appointments with individuals or families they are teaching. 7. Preparation for Bed: Before bed, missionaries have personal time for reflection, prayer, and study. They then prepare for bed and get a good night’s sleep to be ready for the next day’s activities.
